# SpO₂ — the metric the device doesn't see

*Oxygen saturation, ODI, and why a CPAP machine only tells half the story.*

## What CPAP machines measure — and what they don't

A modern CPAP machine measures airflow, leak, pressure, and detects apneas and hypopneas from those signals. What it doesn't measure: blood oxygen saturation.

It's like monitoring traffic on a highway by counting lane markings but never looking at the cars. You can see that something is happening — but not what it causes.

## The O2Ring

Since August 2024 I've been wearing a Wellue O2Ring on my finger every night. The ring measures oxygen saturation (SpO₂) and heart rate via pulse oximetry, every second, all night long.

The first surprise: My ODI — oxygen desaturations per hour — was around 21. AHI was 4. That means: for every apnea the CPAP detected, there were five oxygen drops the device didn't see.

## What the long-term data shows

Over 600+ nights, a clear picture has emerged:

**AHI responds immediately** to config changes. Higher pressure → fewer apneas. That's what CPAP does well.

**ODI barely responds** to config changes. It improved slowly over 21 months — from ~21 to ~14 — but gradually, not in jumps. And regardless of whether I set the pressure from 7 to 10 or from 10 to 14.

The **SpO₂ baseline** (average over the night) has risen from 92.5% to about 93.5%. One percentage point sounds small, but it's a consistent trend over months.

## What this means

CPAP controls the mechanical events — the breathing pauses. But oxygen saturation apparently has its own physiology that isn't fully governed by CPAP pressure alone.

The desaturations may be related to sleep position (right side worse than left), sleep stage (REM vs. NREM), or other factors that CPAP doesn't influence.

## Bottom line

A pulse oximeter costs about €100. It provides information that no CPAP machine can deliver. If you want to seriously understand your sleep apnea therapy, don't just watch the AHI — look at what's happening underneath.

# What AHI really means

*Why sleep medicine's most important number says less than most people think.*

## The standard interpretation

AHI — Apnea-Hypopnea Index. Apneas plus hypopneas per hour of sleep. Below 5: normal. 5–15: mild. 15–30: moderate. Above 30: severe.

That's what every textbook says, what every doctor explains. AHI is the currency of sleep medicine. It drives diagnoses, therapy indications, device approvals.

But after 650 nights of my own data collection, I've learned that AHI has three fundamental problems.

## Problem 1: AHI is not objective

An AHI is the output of an algorithm. Different devices use different algorithms, and the results can diverge dramatically.

My concrete example: On January 14, 2025, I switched from a Löwenstein Prisma Smart to a prismaSMART max. Same pressure settings, same mask, same patient. Median AHI dropped from 4.22 to 0.54 — a factor of 8.

Not because therapy improved. Because the new machine detected central hypopneas ten times less frequently.

An independent polygraph confirmed: the real AHI was 5.6 — not 0.5.

## Problem 2: AHI varies considerably

In my data, AHI fluctuates from night to night by a factor of 3–5, even with a stable configuration. Sleep position, alcohol, medications, the previous night — all influence the value.

A single AHI from a sleep lab night is a sample of one. To get a reliable picture, you need weeks or months of data.

## Problem 3: AHI ignores oxygen saturation

An AHI of 2 sounds harmless. But if that patient drops below 90% SpO₂ ten times per hour, the night isn't harmless. AHI counts events but says nothing about their physiological severity.

In my data, ODI (oxygen desaturations per hour) correlates only weakly with AHI. CPAP pressure controls the apneas — but desaturations have their own dynamic that AHI doesn't capture.

## Bottom line

AHI isn't useless. It's a starting point, a rough indicator. But it's not a measure of night quality, and it's not an objective value. If you want to truly understand your sleep apnea therapy, you need more than a single number — and more than a single device calculating it.

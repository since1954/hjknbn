# The device switch

*A new CPAP machine, an AHI dropping by a factor of 8 — and an independent sleep study showing why.*

## January 2025: Breakdown and replacement

After about five years my Prisma Smart died. The replacement came quickly: a Löwenstein prismaSMART max, the successor model. Same manufacturer, same pressure settings, same mask. On January 14, 2025 — between night 98 and 99 in my database — I switched devices.

What happened next was unexpected.

## The AHI drops through the floor

With the old device my median AHI was 4.22. Stable for months, consistent with the 2023 sleep lab findings.

With the new device: 0.54. Not 4, not 3 — zero point five. A factor-8 drop, overnight.

My first thought: the new device is better. Higher pressure capability, better therapy, fewer events. But two things didn't fit:

The ODI barely changed — median 20.9 before, 16.4 after. Only about 20% decline, and that came gradually over months, not overnight.

And SpO₂ values around the switch: unchanged.

If therapy had truly improved dramatically, oxygen should have improved too. It didn't.

## The explanation: Not the therapy, the algorithm

AHI is not a physical measurement like blood pressure or temperature. It's the output of an algorithm that interprets airflow data. And the prismaSMART max uses a different algorithm than the Prisma Smart.

Central hypopneas showed the most extreme difference: a factor-10 reduction in detected events. The new device saw the same breathing patterns — and classified them differently.

The therapy hadn't improved. The scoring had changed.

## June 2025: Independent confirmation

In June 2025 I wore an independent polygraph device (SOMNOtouch RESP) alongside my CPAP for one night. The result: AHI 5.6 — ten times higher than what the prismaSMART max reported for the same night.

The polygraph confirmed what the data had already suggested: my real AHI under current therapy is 4–6, not 0.5. The positional component was visible too: AHI 9.6 on the right side, 3.2 on the left.

## What this means

A patient reading only the CPAP display would think: AHI 0.5 — perfect therapy. A doctor seeing only the device data would confirm this.

Without independent measurement — without the O2Ring, without my own database, without the comparison across 600+ nights — this discrepancy would have stayed invisible.

This isn't a criticism of Löwenstein. Different algorithms are a reality in sleep medicine. But it's an argument for taking your own data seriously.

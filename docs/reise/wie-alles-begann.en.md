# How it all began

*Fifteen years of sleep apnea, one night in a sleep lab, and the question that changed everything.*

## The diagnosis that changed nothing

In January 2009 I spent my first night in a sleep lab. The diagnosis: obstructive sleep apnea. I received a CPAP machine, put on the mask, and carried on as before.

For fifteen years.

Once a year I visited my pulmonologist, who read out the memory card. "Looks good," was the usual verdict. I had no reason to doubt it. The machine ran, I slept — what more was there to know?

## Klinik Schillerhöhe, December 2023

Late 2023 my pulmonologist sent me for an in-patient polysomnography at Klinik Schillerhöhe, Robert Bosch Hospital. Two nights: one without CPAP (washout), one with.

Without CPAP: AHI 20.6 per hour. Supine: 39.4. Oxygen minimum: 74%. The machine was keeping me alive — without it, the night was a disaster.

With CPAP: AHI around 7. Oxygen minimum: 86%, average 93%. The verdict: "Good therapy outcomes."

Good therapy outcomes. AHI 7. Case closed.

## The question

But what does AHI 7 actually mean? The number describes a single night in an unfamiliar environment, wired head to toe, with a camera pointed at me. What happens on the other 364 nights? Does the AHI fluctuate? Is it sometimes 3 and sometimes 15? And what about the oxygen levels that my CPAP machine doesn't even measure?

In April 2024 I filled out a sleep questionnaire at my doctor's office. Sometime in the weeks that followed, a decision took shape — not a grand gesture, just a quiet resolve: I wanted to collect my own data. Every night. Systematically.

In July 2024 I recorded the first night.
